During my time at the University of Waterloo studying Arts and Business, I participated in a business simulation that allowed me to enrich my critical thinking skills. The simulation required students to work in groups to manage a firm that produces and sells sensors. We were tasked with managing a wide variety of business functions including marketing, production, R&D, and finance.

In the third year of the simulation, my group encountered some difficulties as we fell short of our sales projections and ended up requiring a 13 million dollar emergency bailout. This shortfall happened because two of the competing firms released new products in our strongest market segment. These new products effectively cut our demand in half. We had produced approximately 4000 units of our product which we expected to sell, but at the end of the year we still had about 2000 units left. The excess units caused us to incur significant warehousing costs which in turn caused our company to lose money.

I was asked by my group members to develop a strategy to respond to our poor performance in the previous in-game year. I needed to find a way to return to prosperity so that we could continue investing resources in efficiency measures to make our firm more profitable in the long term.

I thought deeply about the challenge of adjusting our strategy, and developed several tools for helping to create a new strategy. One approach that I developed was to analyze the actions of the other teams, especially the ones that had taken our market share for the first three years of the simulation. By looking at the other teamsâ€™ decisions I was able to gain a better understanding of the strategies they were pursuing which allowed me to better plan our strategy. Another consideration that I included in my strategic planning was the fact that one of the other teams had introduced two of their allotted three new products into the segment where we lost our market share. I realized that this meant they would be vulnerable in other market segments where we would be able to introduce new products and they would not. Another realization that I made was that we had almost enough of our excess product to produce nothing in that market segment for the next year. The vastly reduced production costs for that product allowed me to allocate significant sums of money into the marketing and production optimization of other products which would allow us to make more money into the future.

Ultimately my decision paid off as our team ended up placing first amongst our competition. By realizing that we could not directly compete in the segment where we had lost a significant amount of market share, and allocating resources elsewhere, we were able to gain an edge over our competition that would ultimately lead to our victory. Our company ended up making 144 million dollars in cumulative profit, greater than 50 million dollars more than the second place team.
